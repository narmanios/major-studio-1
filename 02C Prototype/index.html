Traced in Shadow: The Silhouette Sitters &
Diverse Faces of Early America

About

Silhouette Art of Early America is a data visualization project inspired by the work of William Bache, an early 19th-century artist who used a device called a physiognotrace to create nearly 2,000 silhouette portraits. Traveling from Philadelphia to New Orleans and into the Caribbean, Bache captured a wide cross-section of American society. From artisans and merchants to women and people of African descent. His ledger was recently digitized and researched by the Smithsonian’s National Portrait Gallery, and offers a rare glimpse into the lives of everyday people in early American history.

This project transforms those silhouettes into interactive data, mapping where Bache traveled, who sat for him, and what their profiles can tell us about identity, mobility, and representation in early America. By linking visual art with historical data, Silhouette Art of Early America invites viewers to explore these portraits not just as images, but as entries into a larger narrative about the past.

For full collection records, visit the Smithsonian American Art Museum.

Data and images courtesy of the Smithsonian Institution.
